# Fresh Out The Box — Audience Personas

Five portraits of the people we serve. These are composite profiles drawn from the market context, not individuals. Each includes: snapshot, mindset at the point of discovery, the message that lands, and the message that wounds — because in this category, tone-deafness is unforgivable.

---

## Persona 1: Maya

**"The Executive Who Isn't Slowing Down"**

**Snapshot**
52 years old. Corporate VP at a mid-size financial services firm. Chicago. Married, two grown children. Has worn her natural hair in a consistent, professional style for twenty years. First chemotherapy session in three weeks. Has told her direct reports; has not told her broader organization.

**Hair Loss Situation**
Her oncologist told her she'll likely lose her hair within two to four weeks of treatment starting. She has never worn a wig. She has never worn a hat to work. She has ten minutes to get ready on most mornings.

**What She's Tried**
She bought one high-end wig online. It arrived looking beautiful on the model and strange on her. She returned it. She looked at chemo headwear and immediately closed the tab — she described them later as "what they give you in the hospital."

**Trigger Moment**
A colleague at her breast cancer support group mentioned that she'd worn a "hat with built-in hair" to an all-hands meeting and no one knew. Maya pulled out her phone mid-meeting.

**Emotional State at Discovery**
Controlled urgency. She is not panicking — she is problem-solving. She wants to find something that works before her hair goes, not after. She is efficiency-focused and slightly suspicious of anything that feels like false comfort.

**Message That Lands**
"Ready in ten seconds. Looks like your hair." She does not need a story about confidence; she has confidence. She needs a product that respects her time and holds up in a boardroom. Lead with function, let the fashion speak for itself.

**Message That Wounds**
- Any framing that positions her as fragile, scared, or vulnerable
- "You're going through so much — you deserve to feel beautiful." She is not looking for comfort. She is looking for a solution.
- Overlong testimonials about emotional journeys
- Clinical language that reminds her she's a patient when she's trying to feel like an executive

**Her Actual Voice**
*"I don't need it to look like I'm not dealing with something. I just need it to look like me."*

## Persona 2: Diane

**"The One Who Made It and Is Figuring Out What's Next"**

**Snapshot**
61 years old. Retired school principal, now tutoring and volunteering. Birmingham, Alabama. Grandmother of three. Completed chemotherapy eleven months ago. Hair is growing back — soft, about an inch long — but her granddaughter's quinceañera is in six weeks and her hair isn't ready.

**Hair Loss Situation**
Post-treatment regrowth. She looks different than she did before cancer. Her family loves her — but they're also watching her face every time a photo is taken, and she knows it.

**What She's Tried**
She wore wigs through treatment — one synthetic, one human hair she got from a donation program. They were fine for day-to-day but never felt like hers. She's been going without headwear for three months. She's not sure she likes how she looks.

**Trigger Moment**
She saw a photo from a recent family dinner and didn't recognize herself immediately. She sat with that for a week, then started searching online.

**Emotional State at Discovery**
Hopeful but quiet about it. She is not telling her family she's shopping. She is looking for something that will make her look like herself again in a photo that will exist for the next forty years.

**Message That Lands**
"For the days that matter, and the ones that are just Tuesday." The celebration context is real, but she also doesn't want a product that's only for special occasions — she wants to come back to herself in everyday life. Show her real women at milestones and also at grocery stores.

**Message That Wounds**
- Any mention of "survivorship" as an identity — she is not performing survivorship, she is just living
- Comparisons to before-and-after that center the before as a loss
- Anything that implies she should be more grateful or more celebratory
- Language implying hair is beauty, and therefore its absence was an absence of beauty

**Her Actual Voice**
*"I just want to look like myself in the pictures. That's all."*

## Persona 3: Priya

**"The One Who's Been Managing This for Years"**

**Snapshot**
38 years old. UX product manager, fully remote. Seattle. Married, one seven-year-old. Diagnosed with alopecia areata at 34 — began with one bald patch, now covers about 60% of her scalp. Uses scarves most days, a wig for video presentations. Has given this more time and money than she ever expected to.

**Hair Loss Situation**
Chronic, ongoing, unpredictable. She wakes up and some days are fine; others she finds new patches. She has tried: Rogaine (didn't work), PRP injections (partially helped, discontinued), three different wigs, sixteen different hats, and one fiber powder she would like to forget.

**What She's Tried**
Everything, essentially. Her frustration is not with the category — it's with the compromise. The wigs that look good take time; the hats that are comfortable look clinical. She wants the morning to be simple.

**Trigger Moment**
Her seven-year-old asked why she looked different on video calls than in person. She went to bed that night and started Googling "hat with hair attached no wig."

**Emotional State at Discovery**
Tired and specific. She knows what she wants: a product that does what it claims, isn't covered in pink ribbons, and doesn't involve a thirty-minute routine. She will read the full product page. She will look at every photo. She will find the coily-texture halo option or she will leave without buying.

**Message That Lands**
"Finally — a hat that brings its own hair." The simplicity is the message. Show it on diverse hair textures including coily/curly halos. Be specific about the materials, the bamboo lining, the adjustable fit. She's done with vague claims about comfort.

**Message That Wounds**
- Anything that leans into cancer imagery — she does not have cancer and being lumped into that narrative erases her
- Vague "for every woman" language — she needs to see her hair texture, her skin tone, her life
- Testimonials that describe the product as life-changing. She's heard that before.
- Pink. Please.

**Her Actual Voice**
*"I just want to get ready in the morning like everyone else."*

## Persona 4: Sofia

**"The Caregiver Who Wants to Give the Right Thing"**

**Snapshot**
46 years old. Marketing director. Miami. Her younger sister was diagnosed with breast cancer eight months ago and is mid-treatment. Sofia has been the one who drives to appointments, manages schedules, and fields family questions. She wants to do something that actually helps. She is terrified of giving her sister something that makes her feel sick.

**Hair Loss Situation**
Sofia doesn't have hair loss. She is buying for someone who does. She knows her sister is proud, stylish, and will never ask for help directly.

**What She's Tried**
She bought her sister a gift basket from a cancer-support organization. Her sister thanked her and put it on a shelf. She bought a silk pillowcase that was well-received. She is trying to find something that looks like a fashion gift, not a care package.

**Trigger Moment**
She overheard her sister on the phone saying she felt embarrassed to go to a family event. Sofia got off the call and immediately started looking.

**Emotional State at Discovery**
Motivated but worried. She is reading the room carefully. She will scrutinize the copy to make sure it doesn't feel clinical or pitying — because if the packaging feels like a hospital gift shop, she will not buy it, no matter how good the product is.

**Message That Lands**
"The gift that looks like a compliment." Lean on the fashion. The packaging needs to look like something she'd give her sister for a birthday, not a diagnosis. Make it easy to present it as a style gift, not a medical coping tool.

**Message That Wounds**
- "Perfect for your loved one going through cancer treatment" — she'll buy somewhere else
- Any mention of "showing support" in the primary copy — she wants her sister to feel stylish, not supported
- Medical-adjacent language or imagery
- Heavy emotional narrative on the product page — Sofia is in enough of that already

**Her Actual Voice**
*"I want her to open it and feel like I thought she was fabulous, not fragile."*

## Persona 5: Dr. Jasmine Williams

**"The Patient Navigator Who Recommends Products She Trusts"**

**Snapshot**
44 years old. Oncology nurse navigator at a regional cancer center in Atlanta. Works with thirty to forty active patients at a time, guiding them through treatment logistics, emotional transitions, and resource connections. Recommends products regularly — wigs, headwear, skincare for radiation — but only what she can stand behind. Her patients trust her implicitly.

**Situation**
Jasmine is not buying for herself. She is evaluating brands as a trust gatekeeper. If she recommends FOTB, she'll recommend it to dozens of women this year. If the brand feels irresponsible — overclaiming on comfort, making promises about HSA/FSA reimbursement that aren't accurate, or sounding pitying — she will not recommend it. She has seen brands harm patients by raising expectations a product can't meet.

**What She Looks For**
- Clear, accurate product claims (she will read the fine print)
- Accessible pricing with honest reimbursement language ("may be eligible," not "HSA-approved")
- Imagery that reflects her patient population: multiple skin tones, ages, and treatment stages
- A brand that treats her patients as stylish women, not patients in need of uplift
- Referral materials she can hand a patient without embarrassment

**Trigger Moment**
A patient came in wearing a FOTB hat and said it was the first time she'd looked in the mirror and seen herself. Dr. Williams asked for the website.

**Message That Lands**
"Designed with the scalp in mind. Worn because it looks good." Lead with the product science: bamboo/cotton lining, scalp-safe construction, adjustable for changing head shape during treatment. Then the fashion. In that order for clinic materials.

**Message That Wounds**
- Overclaiming on HSA/FSA reimbursement
- Any language that implies the product will cure or reduce psychological distress — that's a medical claim
- Inspirational-poster register ("You are stronger than you know") — she deals in specifics, not platitudes
- Not seeing her patient population reflected in the photography

**Her Actual Voice**
*"If it's going to go in my hand as a recommendation, it needs to be something I'd put my name on."*
