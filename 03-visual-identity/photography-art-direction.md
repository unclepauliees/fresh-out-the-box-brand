# Fresh Out The Box — Photography Art Direction

All photography direction assumes original shoots. No stock imagery. These are shot briefs — descriptions specific enough for a photographer and stylist to execute and for an art director to brief a creative team.

**Overarching directive:** Every image should pass this single test — would a woman who does *not* have hair loss see this image and think "that's a brand I'd consider buying from"? If the answer is no, the image doesn't belong in the brand system.

---

## Representation Requirements (Non-Negotiable)

The following must be structurally represented across any set of six or more brand images. This is not a checklist to hit once and forget — it must hold across every content cycle.

**Skin tones:** Full Fitzpatrick scale representation, specifically ensuring Dark Brown (V) and Deep (VI) are not underrepresented. The coily/natural hair texture variants are disproportionately worn by Black women — they must appear in premium contexts, not only in "diversity" panels.

**Hair textures:** The Remy hair insert collection spans straight, wavy, and coily/curly textures (including 4A–4C coil definitions). Every texture that exists in the product line must appear in hero/editorial images, not just PDP product shots.

**Ages:** The primary audience is 40–65. This age range must be the majority in editorial imagery. Not "diverse ages including one woman over 40" — the 50s and 60s demographic IS the primary customer. Women in this age range with grey or silver inserts should appear in editorial, confidence-forward contexts, not in soft-focus nostalgic framings.

**Head shapes:** Natural variation in head shapes and facial structures. There is no "ideal" head shape for these products — the adjustable fit means they work on varied anatomies, and the imagery must reflect that.

**Treatment stage representation:** At least 30% of lifestyle imagery should include women who are evidently in treatment (no eyelashes or eyebrows, visibly tired, wearing medical-adjacent jewelry). These women should appear in normal, stylish, everyday contexts — not in hospital settings, not in soft lighting that signals vulnerability.

## Shot Categories

### Category 1: The Getting-Dressed Portrait

**Brief:** A woman at her mirror, putting on or having just put on the FOTB hat. The key visual detail: she is in the act of becoming ready, not in the act of managing a medical accessory. The expression should be neutral moving toward satisfaction — not a performed smile, not a grimace. She looks like herself.

**Lighting:** Natural window light, morning. Warm, directional, not diffused into flatness. Shadows are fine — they read as real, not as commercial-beauty bright.

**Set/Environment:** Her bedroom or bathroom. Lived-in. Well-curated but not staged to within an inch of its life. A few things on the dresser. Her actual wardrobe visible.

**Wardrobe:** Fashion-forward, not aspirationally luxurious. A blazer she actually owns. Earrings that are a statement. The hat is part of the outfit, not the focal point of the frame.

**What to avoid:** Before/after framing. The "before" should never exist in the brand's visual universe.

**Specific representation:** This shot category must include at least: one woman, late 50s, Dark Brown skin tone, coily-texture halo insert; one woman, 40s, South Asian complexion, wavy insert; one woman, 60s, white/silver hair insert, visible facial features suggesting current or recent treatment.

---

### Category 2: Out in the World

**Brief:** A woman going about a normal life moment wearing FOTB. Not posed for the camera — caught in motion. Walking out of a building, getting coffee, in a meeting, at a restaurant with a friend, picking up a prescription (she does not look like a patient at this moment — she looks like someone who has somewhere to be).

**Lighting:** Real-world light — mixed, imperfect, sometimes contrasty. Not perfect product photography light.

**Expression:** Present, in-the-moment. Not looking at the camera unless the shot specifically requires it. The hat is incidental — part of her, not the story.

**Scale:** Medium shots to wide. We are seeing her in context, not studying her face. The context is what tells the story.

**Key shot to capture in this category:** Two women at a café table, mid-conversation, one wearing FOTB. They are talking — about anything except hair loss. The hat is background. The connection is foreground.

---

### Category 3: Detail and Material

**Brief:** Close-up on the hat, the lining, the insert, the hair texture. These shots exist to show craftsmanship and material honesty. They are product shots, but they should feel editorial — laid flat on a stone or wood surface, or held at an angle with natural shadow.

**Key details to capture:**
- The bamboo-cotton lining: up close, showing the woven texture, the softness implied by the material
- The hair insert: the real texture, movement, density — shot to look like hair, not like a wig
- The halo/insert mechanism: how it attaches to the shell — shot to look modular and clean, not mechanical
- The adjustable band: open and showing the adjustment range

**Surface/Background:** Warm-toned surfaces. Stone, warm linen, or matte wood. Not white sweep or medical-blank backgrounds.

**Lighting:** Soft directional. Long shadows that give dimension to the texture.

**Color:** Detail shots should be monochromatic or near-monochromatic in palette — showing the Cognac/Espresso/Warm Sand range of the product against complementary surfaces.

---

### Category 4: The Founder Frame

**Brief:** Zahara Acevedo. Use sparingly — the brand should not become founder-dependent. When used, she should appear as the subject of a profile moment, not as a symbol. She is the woman who built the thing. Show her like that: thoughtful, capable, present.

**Tone:** Interview-style. She is talking or thinking, not posing. The environment should be her actual workspace or home — functional, real, warm.

**What to avoid:** Any framing that invites the viewer to feel sorry for her. The history of her treatment is hers to share; the visual frame should be its aftermath — a woman who built something, period.

---

### Category 5: The Unboxing

**Brief:** The product being unboxed. The box opening. The hat and insert revealed in branded tissue. Hands on tissue paper. The insert card being read.

**Tone:** Gift-level. This should feel like opening something you're pleased to have received — the same emotional register as unboxing a well-chosen piece of jewelry.

**Lighting:** Warm daylight. A table by a window.

**Hands:** Real hands. Manicure optional but not impeccably styled. The hands of a person opening a gift, not a set-dressed prop.

**Shot sequence to capture (for use in social/email):**
1. Box closed, branding visible on lid
2. Lid lifting — first reveal
3. Inside lid copy visible
4. Tissue paper with logo seal visible
5. Hat revealed, insert beside it in the tissue
6. Insert card in hand

## Editing and Post-Production Direction

**Skin retouching:** None. No smoothing, no blemish removal, no digital hair restoration in skin. The brand does not erase the physical reality of its customers — doing so in post undermines the brand's commitment to honest representation.

**Color grade:** Warm. A slight golden cast in the highlights; shadows pull toward a warm mahogany rather than cold blue. Never cool, never desaturated. Think warm morning editorial, not Instagram clean aesthetic.

**Sharpness:** The hat and hair detail should be sharp. The background can be lightly softened but should not be artificially blurred into fashion-catalog mushiness.

**Diversity of context:** Do not color-grade different women differently. A common error is to grade images of Black women with higher contrast or lower brightness. One grade, applied consistently across all images.

---

## What Never Appears in Brand Photography

- Hospital rooms, treatment centers, IV poles, medical equipment
- Pink ribbons, pink anything as a dominant color
- Crying, expressions of despair, vulnerability
- "Before" comparisons (bare heads positioned as the "problem" visual)
- Soft-focus or dreamy processing applied to older women (implies frailty)
- Product floating alone on a white background (reads as medical device catalog)
- Anyone looking pityingly at the woman wearing the product
- Dramatic backlit silhouettes with inspirational-poster staging
