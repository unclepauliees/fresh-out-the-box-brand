# Fresh Out The Box — Lexicon

Words we use. Words we never use. With rationale.

This document is not about avoiding difficulty — it is about accuracy. Every word on the "never use" list earned its place not because it's too blunt, but because it is imprecise, borrowed from a different context, or carries connotations that contradict what this brand is.

---

## Words We Use

### About the person

| Use | Because |
|---|---|
| **Woman / women** | She is a person, not a condition. Default noun throughout. |
| **Customer** | In transactional contexts. Normal commercial language. |
| **She / her** | Our primary audience is women; use clearly without hedging in consumer copy. Inclusive copy for clinical or wholesale materials where audience is broader. |
| **Person** | In contexts where gender specificity is unnecessary or unhelpful. |

### About the experience

| Use | Because |
|---|---|
| **Hair loss** | Accurate, medical-neutral, no connotation baggage. |
| **Shedding** | Accurate, used in dermatology, not clinical-sounding in consumer contexts. |
| **Treatment** | General, honest, covers chemo/radiation/immunotherapy without requiring specifics. |
| **Getting dressed** | Centers the ordinary. The act of getting dressed is the brand's territory — protect it. |
| **Morning routine** | Practical, universal, non-medical. |
| **Sensitive scalp** | Specific enough to be useful; general enough to apply across conditions. |
| **Regrowth** | Clear, positive directionally without being promissory about timeline. |

### About the product

| Use | Because |
|---|---|
| **Hat** | It is a hat. Not an "accessory" (too vague), not a "headpiece" (slightly bridal), not a "device" (medical). A hat. |
| **Halo / hair halo** | Product-specific term for the Remy hair insert. Elegant, referential, not medical. |
| **Insert** | The removable hair piece, in functional copy. |
| **Shell** | The hat body without the insert, in functional copy. |
| **Remy hair** | Always spell out Remy (not "human hair" alone, which sounds clinical; not "natural hair," which can cause confusion re: natural hair styling). |
| **Bamboo-cotton lining** | Specific material claim. Always name both materials. |
| **Adjustable fit** | Functional claim. Accurate and useful. |
| **Modular** | For technical or clinic-facing copy explaining the shell-and-halo architecture. |

### About the brand promise

| Use | Because |
|---|---|
| **Feel like yourself** | The brand promise. Direct, personal, not aspirational toward a better self — toward her actual self. |
| **Look like you** | Variant of the above. Specific to appearance without implying appearance is the only goal. |
| **Confidence** | Acceptable in limited doses — this is what the brand restores. Avoid making it the headline; let the product carry the weight. |
| **Ease / easy** | Functional claim for the "ready" pillar. Do not overuse — when everything is described as easy, nothing is. |

## Words We Never Use

### Cancer clichés

| Never use | Why |
|---|---|
| **Battle / fight / fought** | Implies cancer is an enemy to be defeated; positions people who die as having "lost." The brand does not traffic in war metaphors. |
| **Warrior** | Same problem. Also increasingly aware as hollow. |
| **Survivor / survivor journey** | "Survivor" is a clinical and community self-identifier — it is hers to use about herself, not ours to use about her. "Survivor journey" is marketing language that reduces a lived experience to a narrative arc. |
| **Brave / bravery / courageous** | Implies that dealing with illness is a character achievement. It is a circumstance. |
| **Strong / strength** | The same problem, slightly more subtle. Also: she may not feel strong. We should not demand that she be. |
| **You've got this** | Cheerleader register. The brand is a stylish friend, not a pep rally. |
| **Stay strong** | Same register problem. |
| **Everything happens for a reason** | Toxic positivity. Never appropriate. |
| **In this together** | Overused since 2020; rings false in brand copy. |

### Pity language

| Never use | Why |
|---|---|
| **Going through so much / so hard** | Acknowledges difficulty but in a way that centers the brand's awareness of her suffering. She doesn't need us to know how hard it is. |
| **Deserve** | "You deserve to feel beautiful" implies she might not, absent our product. She does not need to earn anything. |
| **Bless** | Any register of blessing or grace in a consumer context reads as pity. |
| **Those affected by** | Medical pamphlet language. She is a customer. |
| **Loved ones** | Soft and vague. If talking to gift buyers, name the relationship or use her name. |

### Hair = worth

| Never use | Why |
|---|---|
| **Crown / mane** | Both position hair as a woman's defining feature or source of power. If hair is her crown, losing it is losing her crown. We do not build products on that premise. |
| **Her glory** | Same problem, religious register. |
| **Complete / whole** | "Feel complete again" implies hair loss made her incomplete. It did not. |
| **Restore your beauty** | Beauty is not what was lost. Self-recognition is. Different claim. |
| **Feel beautiful** | See above. Also: the product makes her look like herself — that's a different bar than "beautiful." |

### Medical / clinical in consumer contexts

| Never use (in consumer copy) | Why | Use instead |
|---|---|---|
| **Cranial prosthesis** | Accurate in insurance/reimbursement documentation; othering in consumer context. | Hat, headwear |
| **Therapeutic** | Medical claim. Not supportable without clinical evidence. | "Designed for sensitive scalps" |
| **Treatment aid** | Same. | Describe the product itself |
| **Chemotherapy cap** | Accurate but clinical; in consumer copy this frames the product as a medical device. | Hat, FOTB hat |
| **Patient** | She is a customer, not a patient, in consumer contexts. | Woman, customer, she |
| **Diagnosis** | Not relevant in consumer copy. | If context requires it, reference "hair loss" or "during treatment." |
| **Medically indicated** | Legal/insurance territory. | Remove entirely from consumer copy. |

### Aspirational excess

| Never use | Why |
|---|---|
| **Journey** | Overused. Also implies a narrative arc she may not be experiencing. |
| **Transform / transformation** | The product does not transform her. It helps her look like herself. Accurate framing matters. |
| **Empower / empowerment** | Delegated power is not the same as capability a person already has. We do not empower. We make a hat. |
| **Uplift** | Same problem. Also sounds like a bra. |
| **Reclaim** | Has become category noise in wellness. Also implies she lost ownership of herself, which is not the brand's framing. |
| **Own your look** | The possessive framing is fine; "own" as a verb imperative reads as social-media register. |

## Edge Cases

**"Confidence"** — Use sparingly, and never as the headline claim. The brand *restores* confidence as an outcome, not a product feature. "Restoring confidence" (tagline use) is appropriate. "This hat gives you confidence" crosses into a different kind of promise.

**"Real"** — Acceptable when referring to Remy hair (it is literally real human hair) or to representation (real women, real textures). Avoid using it as a modifier for emotions ("feel real joy").

**"Natural"** — Acceptable for hair texture and appearance claims. Do not use as a wellness/lifestyle modifier ("a more natural you").

**"Normal"** — A careful one. "Look normal" is not the goal — "look like yourself" is more accurate. However, "morning routine back to normal" or "a normal morning" are acceptable in the "Ready" pillar because they're about logistics, not appearance.

**Humor** — The brand is warm and occasionally knowing, but jokes about hair loss — even gentle ones — are off-limits unless coming from a real customer or the founder herself, in their own voice. The brand does not joke about the condition.

**"HSA-eligible"** — Never as a standalone claim. Always: "may be eligible for HSA/FSA reimbursement" + "verify with your plan administrator." See `voice-and-tone.md` clinic context for full hedged language.
