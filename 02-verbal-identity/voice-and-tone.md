# Fresh Out The Box — Voice & Tone

---

## The Voice (What Doesn't Change)

Voice is the brand's consistent character — it doesn't shift by channel or audience. These four qualities are always present.

### Warm
We speak with genuine care that comes from lived experience, not from a brand handbook. The warmth is specific, not performed — it shows up in the words we choose and what we don't overexplain.

*Sounds like:* "The lining is bamboo because that's what doesn't irritate a sensitive scalp." Not: "We care deeply about your comfort journey."

### Direct
We say what we mean. We do not soften product claims into vagueness, pad sentences to seem warmer, or bury the point under qualifiers. This is a sign of respect — we are talking to adults.

*Sounds like:* "It takes ten seconds." Not: "It's designed to be incredibly easy and quick to put on, making your morning so much simpler."

### Candid
We acknowledge the real situation — hair loss, treatment, the hard mornings — without making it the center of gravity. We name it when it's useful and move on. We do not perform sensitivity by tiptoeing, and we do not perform strength by pretending it's easy.

*Sounds like:* "Hair loss is disorienting. This is the hat that makes getting dressed work anyway." Not: "We know you're going through so much right now."

### Fashion-Literate
We speak to taste, style, and the joy of a well-made thing. Not in the breathless register of trend content — in the way a stylish friend talks about a find: specific, a little proprietary, like she's genuinely pleased you'll get to know about it.

*Sounds like:* "The cognac brim is the one that works with everything." Not: "Versatile warm-toned headwear perfect for all occasions."

## Tone (What Flexes)

Tone shifts by context, audience, and emotional moment. The voice stays the same; the register adjusts.

---

### Context 1: Product Page

**Tone:** Confident, specific, editorial.

This is where the fashion-forward positioning is most explicit. The product page is a buying surface — it should make her want to own it, trust the details, and check out.

**What this sounds like:**
Lead with an image-adjacent headline that gives the product a point of view. Follow with a sentence that names the material or texture truth. Then: fit, ease, and the specific hair texture this variant is designed for.

**Example:**

> **The Cedar**
> A structured brim with a camel-colored interior and a deep brunette human-hair halo. The kind of hat that gets compliments in line at the coffee shop.
>
> Adjustable fit. Bamboo-cotton lining. Remy hair insert included, machine-washable. Ships assembled — ready to wear out of the box.
>
> Available in: chestnut, ebony, ash blonde, silver-grey. Coily and wavy texture options.

**What to avoid:** Explaining the medical context on the primary product page. Let it live in the FAQ. The product page is a fashion moment, not an FAQ.

---

### Context 2: First-Shed Moment Email

**Tone:** Quiet, specific, briefly honest, then practical.

This is the hardest context to get right. This email goes to someone who just experienced or is about to experience significant hair shedding. She is looking at her brush. She is at a threshold.

The email must:
- Acknowledge the moment without dwelling on it
- Move quickly to what's useful
- Not perform empathy in a way that makes her feel surveilled or categorized
- End with a clear, easy action

**What this sounds like:**

> Subject: For the mornings ahead
>
> It's a lot, what's happening right now.
>
> You don't have to be okay about it. You also don't have to figure out a whole new morning routine to look like yourself.
>
> Here are the three styles our customers reach for first.
>
> [Product tiles]
>
> Take your time. When you're ready, we're here.

**What to avoid:**
- "You've got this" — cheerleader register
- "We know this is hard" — she knows; saying it out loud in an email can feel like a brand intrusion into a private moment
- Overly long emotional preamble before getting to the product — it reads as performative
- Emotional language about hair being a symbol of anything ("your mane," "your crown")

---

### Context 3: Social

**Tone:** Specific, light, occasionally playful, fashion-forward.

Social is where we're least clinical and most like a brand someone wants to follow because it has a point of view about aesthetics, style, and life. One idea per post. No over-explaining.

**What this sounds like:**

Caption with a product flat lay:
> Bamboo inside, brunette halo outside. The Cedar. Ten seconds.

Caption with a woman at a table, hat on, coffee in hand:
> Tuesday morning. Dressed. Coffee. Good.

Caption with a quote:
> "I forgot I was wearing it."
> — That's the whole point.

**What to avoid:**
- Hashtag overloading with condition-specific terms (#cancerlife, #chemohead, etc.) — follow where the audience is; don't create a medical content bucket
- Inspirational-poster text
- Any post centered on a "before" (hair loss) and "after" (hat on) — this framing treats hair loss as the problem and the product as the fix; she is not a before

---

### Context 4: Clinic One-Pager

**Tone:** Professional, specific, hedged where required. More formal register; still warm.

This goes into a patient navigator's hand. She is a healthcare professional. She needs:
1. Clear product description
2. Materials and construction claims she can stand behind
3. Pricing that is honest
4. Reimbursement language that is hedged (never promissory)
5. Contact information and referral path

**What this sounds like:**

> **FRESH OUT THE BOX**
> Fashion-forward headwear with integrated Remy human hair.
>
> Bamboo-cotton interior lining designed for scalp sensitivity. Adjustable inner band accommodates treatment-related head circumference changes. Remy hair insert is removable and machine-washable.
>
> Hero kit: $179 | Individual styles: $89–129
>
> HSA/FSA Note: Some patients may be eligible for partial reimbursement. Eligibility depends on individual plan. We recommend patients verify with their plan administrator. We provide itemized receipts upon request.

**What to avoid:**
- "Cranial prosthesis" — use only if the patient or navigator raises it in an insurance context
- Emotional language (this is a professional referral document)
- Unhedged HSA/FSA claims ("HSA-eligible" as a flat statement)

---

### Context 5: Customer Service

**Tone:** Practical, warm, never robotic. Does not perform cheerfulness.

Customer service interactions are often post-purchase friction moments — wrong size, shipping delay, product question. The tone should feel like a real person who actually cares about the outcome, not a scripted response.

**What this sounds like:**

> Hi [Name] — thanks for reaching out. That's not how it should arrive.
>
> We're sending a replacement today. You'll get a tracking number in the next hour.
>
> Is there anything else going on with the order?

**What to avoid:**
- "I'm so sorry you're experiencing this" — sounds automated
- "Thank you for your patience during this difficult time" — the double meaning is obvious and unfortunate in this brand context
- "Have a wonderful day!" — mismatched affect for someone who may be on a hard week
- Over-apologizing in a way that shifts the interaction from a solvable problem to an emotional event

## Voice in Summary: The Test

Before publishing any piece of copy, ask:

1. Would a nurse write it? (If yes, rewrite.)
2. Would it fit in a hospital pamphlet? (If yes, rewrite.)
3. Does it treat the reader as someone who needs uplift? (If yes, rewrite.)
4. Does it say anything about her being strong, brave, or in a journey? (If yes, rewrite.)
5. Does it make the product sound medical or therapeutic? (If yes, rewrite.)
6. Would a woman who doesn't have hair loss still want this product from this copy? (If no, consider whether the fashion framing is strong enough.)
